Vendor Performance Analysis

End-to-end analysis and ETL-driven dashboard that identified vendor concentration risks, inventory carrying issues, and pricing opportunities — enabling procurement and operations teams to prioritize supplier negotiations and reduce working capital exposure...

Problem Statement

A mid-sized retail inventory dataset (procurement, sales, stock, and vendor master records) showed inconsistent reporting, wide variance in freight and pricing, and unclear vendor contribution to procurement dollars. The business needed a clear, data-driven view of which vendors and SKUs were driving costs, where capital was locked in unsold inventory, and whether bulk purchasing actually delivered unit-cost savings.

Solution Approach

    As the project lead, I was responsible for :

  • Building a reproducible ETL pipeline to consolidate and clean procurement, sales and inventory tables.
  • Implementing data-quality checks and reconciliation steps so KPIs were audit-ready.
  • Producing an interactive dashboard and a set of evidence-based recommendations to improve procurement decisions, pricing and inventory policies.

    Technical & Analytical approach

    1. Data ingestion & ETL

  • Connected to the inventory SQLite database and extracted the vendor/sales view (VSS) as the canonical table.
  • Built Python ETL scripts (Pandas + SQL) to standardize columns, handle missing/zero values, convert datatypes, and store a cleaned dataset ready for analysis.

    • 2. Data quality & validation

  • Implemented automated validation checks (null thresholds, negative-value detection, type checks) and row-level filters to remove inconsistent transactions prior to analysis.
  • Created reconciliation queries that compare purchase vs. sales aggregates to ensure traceability.

    • 3. Feature engineering & KPIs

  • Engineered business metrics such as GrossProfit, ProfitMargin, StockTurnover, TotalPurchaseDollars, and PurchaseContribution% (per vendor).
  • Computed vendor-level aggregates (purchase dollars, sales dollars, gross profit) for ranking and contribution analysis.

    • 4. Exploratory data analysis & statistics

  • Performed summary statistics and outlier detection to identify extreme freight and price values.
  • Ran correlation analysis to measure relationships (e.g., PurchaseQuantity vs TotalSalesQuantity) and used inferential statistics — two-sample t-tests and 95% confidence intervals — to compare profit margins across vendor cohorts.

    • 5. Business analysis & segmentation

  • Segmented vendors into top-performing and low-performing groups and analyzed differences in margin, turnover and risk exposure.
  • Measured the effect of order size on unit cost (bulk vs small orders) and identified thresholds where unit cost fell significantly.

    • 6. Visualization & reporting

  • Built exploratory visualizations (scatter plots, box plots, histograms) using Matplotlib & Seaborn for the analysis notebook.
  • Designed a production-ready Power BI dashboard with drill-downs, DAX KPIs and interactive filters to let stakeholders explore vendor contribution, unsold inventory, and pricing levers.

    • 7. Recommendations & documentation

  • Documented recurring data issues and remediation steps (root-cause notes), and packaged findings into an executive summary and action list.

Key Insights Discovered

  • High variance in freight and cost: Freight values spanned a massive range (example: 0.09 → 257,032.07), indicating inconsistent logistics or occasional bulk shipment outliers that require policy standardization.
  • Profitability anomalies: Gross profit values included negative extremes (min observed ≈ -52,002.78), suggesting pricing or discounting issues on certain SKUs.
  • Stock turnover extremes: Stock turnover ranged from 0 up to ~274.5 — showing a mix of deadstock and very fast-moving SKUs requiring differentiated inventory policies.
  • Strong procurement-sales alignment: Purchase quantity vs total sales quantity showed near-perfect aggregate correlation (~0.999) — confirming that procurement broadly follows demand, but suggesting opportunities at SKU/vendor level to improve allocation.
  • Bulk purchasing advantage: Bulk/large orders had substantially lower unit cost — up to ~72% unit cost reduction compared with small orders — highlighting opportunities for negotiated bulk discounts and reorder point optimization.
  • Margin segmentation insight: Confidence-interval analysis revealed that low-selling vendors sometimes show higher margin intervals relative to top-sellers (e.g., top-vendor margin CI around 30.7–31.6%), indicating that high margin alone does not equal high revenue — an important nuance for procurement strategy.

Result / Impact

  • Cost Saving Opportunities: Pinpointed vendors whose cost structures (freight + purchase price) could be renegotiated, with potential for reduced spend.
  • Vendor Optimization: Helped highlight which vendors to prioritize vs underperformers, enabling leaner vendor management.
  • Actionable Visibility: Dashboard enabled transparent views of vendor performance for regular review.
  • Business Recommendations: Suggested strategies like bulk discount leverage, vendor consolidation, and optimizing inventory turnover per vendor.
